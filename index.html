Fragmentation has become the default operating model. Security responsibilities remain stubbornly divided across departments, agencies, sectors, and jurisdictions. The Fragmented Protection Model

Queensland's cybersecurity "model" isn't a deliberate strategy; it's simply an accumulation. Over time, departments and sectors independently built security functions, each aligned with their own mandates and budgets. Crucially, these functions were never integrated. Despite earnest attempts at cross-cutting coordination, they consistently failed to overcome entrenched resistance, significant cost constraints, and fiercely competing operational priorities.

In practice, each agency manages its own security posture. This includes procurement, compliance, incident response, workforce and standards. Agencies differ widely in what they prioritise and how they measure success. Some align with international frameworks. Others operate informally or follow minimum legal requirements.

The Cyber Coordination Office, created in 2027, lacked authority. It offered guidance but could not mandate action. Likewise, the Security Standards Framework (2029) was unevenly adopted. Without legislative backing, it became one of many competing references rather than a unifying instrument.

There is no shared threat intelligence platform. Information sharing is voluntary and often delayed. Alerts are distributed through sectoral channels, with no central body compiling patterns or escalating systemic risks. Large agencies may have their own intelligence functions, but these rarely extend beyond their operational perimeter.

Incident response varies. Each organisation has internal protocols. Cross-agency incidents trigger improvised coordination phone calls, email chains, temporary working groups. Exercises are rare, and there is no formal whole-of-government simulation program.

Investment flows exclusively through departmental budgets, with no pooled funding for shared capabilities. Workforce planning occurs within deep silos, leading to frustratingly duplicated roles and critical talent gaps. Security expertise clusters around high-profile departments, while regional and remote offices are left to operate with minimal specialised support, exacerbating existing vulnerabilities.

Positives of the Fragmented Model

  • Operational familiarity: Agencies manage security through known structures without disruptive governance reform.
  • Tailored protection: Security approaches match operational needs of departments and sectors.
  • Implementation autonomy: Entities can act on priorities without waiting for whole-of-government readiness.
  • Political feasibility: Fragmented model avoids major institutional reform during complex transitions.
  • Specialisation: Domain-specific capabilities allow deep expertise in areas like defence, health or transport.
  • Regulatory clarity within silos: Compliance is manageable within discrete jurisdictions.
  • Existing momentum: Agencies with strong leadership continue progressing without external interference.
  • Local adaptation: Councils and regional bodies develop context-sensitive approaches.

Challenges of the Fragmented Model

  • Trust fragility : Attacks on information credibility can paralyse distributed coordination.
  • Coordination latency: Lack of central command slows response under uncertainty.
  • Implementation gaps: Resource-poor actors may fall short despite shared frameworks.
  • Accountability diffusion: Shared models risk ambiguity in crisis attribution and recovery.
  • Complex onboarding: Smaller players face barriers navigating security certification systems.
